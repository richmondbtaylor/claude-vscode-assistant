## 🧱 THE 7 LEGO BRICK FRAMEWORK (Most important tactical framework)

Every short form video is made of exactly 7 components. Find the top performers in your niche, explode them into these 7 pieces, hold the world-class ones constant, improve on the weak ones, restack into your own formation, and post.

1. **Topic** — What is the video actually about?
2. **Angle** — What's the interesting/shocking/useful frame on that topic?
3. **Hook Structure** — The 3 sub-elements: Visual Hook + Text Hook + Spoken Hook (must align)
4. **Story Structure** — How is the information laid out? Case study, listicle, breakdown, personal narrative, etc.
5. **Visual Format** — Split screen, full screen, circle cam, vlog POV, in-world, etc.
6. **Key Visuals** — A-roll, B-roll, graphics — the actual footage/imagery used
7. **Audio** — Music and sound effects

**For beginners:** Topic and angle change every video. Hold hook structure, story structure, and visual format constant by copying what's already working. Don't try to invent new formats until you've closed the gap on fundamentals.

**For advanced:** Once you have the fundamentals, look at those 7 buckets in your cohort and find where you can be *radically different* on just one of them. That's your first-mover advantage.

## 🪝 HOOKS — The Complete Breakdown

### The 3 Components (All must align)
- **Visual Hook** — What's shown on screen (seen before anything else — speed of light vs. speed of sound)
- **Text Hook** — Title text overlaid (NOT just captions — big contextual words at top/bottom)
- **Spoken Hook** — What's actually said verbally

Misalignment between these = confusion. Confusion = churn. They must all point at the same thing.

### The Hook Formula: 3 Things
**1. Visual Stop (Thumb Stopper)**
Make the visual stun the viewer. They don't need to comprehend it instantly — they just need to pause. Contrast is the mechanism. High motion, high color, high contrast are the three visual attributes. Psychology: the brain is hardwired to detect motion (deer in the woods analogy). A static bedroom or car shot doesn't stop anyone. Motion does.

**2. Context**
Set the context in the first sentence. The brain learns top-down: broad context → how → examples → learning. If you drop someone mid-funnel with no context ("jumped was crazy") they're lost. Context creates the frame for everything that follows.

**3. Contrast**
The difference between what they *expect* to see/hear vs. what you actually show them. This is what makes people lean in.

**Two types of contrast:**
- **Direct Contrast:** "Most people think X. But the truth is Y." — You lay out the common belief explicitly, then flip it. This is more powerful because you're creating a 200-point swing (from +100 to -100) rather than a 100-point swing (from 0 to -100).
- **Implied Contrast:** Make a wild contrarian statement without stating the common belief first. Less powerful because the viewer has to do the mental work of filling in what the common belief was.

**The spectrum math:** If you lean them toward +100 (common belief) and then flip them to -100 (your contrarian take), that's a distance of 200. If you start at 0 and flip to -100, it's only a distance of 100. Always state the common belief first for maximum contrast impact.

## 📝 SPOKEN HOOK PRINCIPLES

- **Absorption Rate** is the KPI — not word count, not clarity alone. Can they absorb 100% of what you said? If you say 8 words and they understand all 8 with certainty, that's 100% absorption.
- **Active vs. passive voice.** "The dog jumped" vs. "the jump was made by the dog." Fewer words, more direct.
- Cut fluff, but don't cut words that add clarity. Sometimes you *need* more words to maintain absorption.
- Every sentence should open one clear question in the viewer's head. Answer that question in the next sentence. That answer opens another question. Repeat. If the first line opens multiple equally-weighted questions, it's too vague.

---

## 🖼️ TEXT ON SCREEN — Two Types

**Captions:** Word-by-word oscillating text. Standard styled closed captions.

**Title Text:** Larger contextual words that aren't necessarily what's being said verbatim. Example: "Stop making your hooks like this" appearing at top while you talk about hooks. This is the context/curiosity gap layer.

The title text helps the viewer triangulate — they see the visual, read the title text, and hear the words. All three confirming each other = high absorption, locked in.

**Thumbnails for short form (underrated):** When a video gets shared in DMs/group chats, the video shows as a static thumbnail — not playing. The click rate on that thumbnail determines if the DM chain actually drives views. Most people wildly underestimate this. Design your covers to work as standalone images.

## 🎬 STORY STRUCTURE FRAMEWORK

The "question-answer chain":
- Line 1 opens one dominant question
- Line 2 answers it — which opens another question
- Line 3 answers that — which opens another
- Continue all the way through

If at any point you read a line and multiple questions of equal strength pop up, the line needs to be rewritten for clarity.

**The two dopamine hit framework:** Get someone to 2 dopamine hits before they'd naturally churn and they're locked in for the rest. 0 hits = bounce immediately. 1 hit = maybe. 2 hits = they think "there could be 20 more, I have to keep watching."

**For educational content:** A dopamine hit = telling them something non-obvious and tactically usable that will solve a real problem when implemented.

**Comprehension is a decaying graph:** Starts at 100% when they hit play. Every word, frame, and pixel can only keep it the same or push it down. When comprehension hits a threshold, they bounce. That's why the first 5-10 seconds of visuals need to be extremely tight with what you're saying.

## 🎞️ HOTTEST FORMAT RIGHT NOW (Jan 2026)

**Personal Narrative-Driven Visual Storytelling** is by far the hottest format and will remain so. Here's why it's winning:

The content format evolution:
1. **Era 1:** Transactional/listicle formats — "5 best AI tools." These got saturated because anyone could make them. AI will replace these entirely.
2. **Era 2:** Case study breakdowns — "This person achieved X in Y way." Added original context, harder to copy.
3. **Era 3 (Now):** Personal narrative storytelling — "Here's what happened to me. Here's what I tried. It failed. Here's what actually worked. Take it or leave it."

Why it's sticky: lived experiences are by definition one-of-one. You can copy the storytelling formula but you can't copy the actual story. They're AI-proof in a way no other format is.

**Power move:** Combine numbered series (30 lessons in 30 days) + personal narrative. The human brain is a puzzle machine — if you see Day 7 of 10 you want to go back and find Day 1-6. This forces bingeability by creating a completion loop. Downside: you have to actually finish the series or you break the promise.

## 📺 YOUTUBE — THE COMPLETE BREAKDOWN

### Why YouTube Wins for Business (Content Minutes Framework)
Every minute of content someone watches = 1 block. Blocks = trust. Trust = purchases.

- A $97 product might take 12 blocks to buy
- A $10,000 product might take 400 blocks
- A 25-minute YouTube video with 10-minute AVD = 10 blocks per rep
- A 60-second short with 30-second average watch = 0.5 blocks per rep
- That means 20 shorts watched = 1 YouTube video in terms of trust accrual

YouTube also compounds because viewers *chose* to be there (intent-based), vs. shorts being served (feed-based). The long rope also matters — at minute 19 of a podcast, you have a 90-second window to breathe. On short form, you have 2.5 seconds before they bounce.

### YouTube Is a Packaging Game
The content doesn't matter if they don't click. YouTube shows your thumbnail for free — but only if they click is it worth anything. The title and thumbnail are the most important thing you make. Do them FIRST, when you're freshest.

**The Psychology of the YouTube Click:**
1. Scroll → eye catches thumbnail → visual attention locks
2. Read the title → looking for keywords that trigger a "desire loop" (solve a pain, FOMO about missing a solution, or a desirable benefit)
3. Eyes go back to thumbnail → thumbnail must *amplify* the desire established by the title, not repeat it
4. Click
5. First 5 seconds of video must confirm the desire loop immediately (not bait-and-switch)
6. First dopamine hit within 30-60 seconds

**Key rule:** Clickbait ≠ bad. You WANT to bait the click. What you don't want is bait-and-switch. Bait and give. Never bait and switch.

**Title and thumbnail roles are different:**
- Title: Triggers the desire loop with keywords
- Thumbnail: Amplifies and elevates that desire through a different angle/image — NOT repeating the same words

**Biggest YouTube mistake:** Doing the packaging at the end after you're mentally exhausted from the edit. Wrong order. The packaging IS the most important part. Do it first.

### YouTube Analytics — How to Actually Use Them
He barely uses analytics because data is *confirmatory*, not *directional*. Inputs matter more than outputs. But when he does look:

**1 out of 10 ranking:** Where does this video rank relative to your last 10 by views? 1 = best, 10 = worst.
- **1-3 out of 10:** Study what you did and try to replicate it
- **4-6 out of 10:** Probably fine — smaller TAM idea, not a failure
- **7-10 out of 10:** Switch the title/thumbnail immediately, as that's the only thing you can change in real-time

**CTR** (click-through rate on thumbnail impressions) is noisy — can be high while the video underperforms and vice versa.

**Business game metric:** If you're playing Game 5, you don't care about raw views. You care about **dollars per view** or **on-target views that convert to offers**. A video with 300 views that generates $300k is infinitely more valuable than 5,000 views that generates $50k.

### The Algorithm Explained
1. You post → YouTube transcribes video in real time → builds a topic map
2. Creates a test sample pool of ~200 people (mix of existing subscribers + new)
3. Blasts video to sample pool
4. Measures: did they watch/engage *more than usual*, same, or less?
5. If more than usual → builds new sample pool 10x bigger (2,000 people with similar profile)
6. Cascades up from there at each order of magnitude
7. If neutral or negative → throttled immediately (that's why bad videos get 200 views total)

**What this means strategically:** Your existing audience is the seed of every distribution cascade. If your audience is built from broad/mixed topics, the seed data is muddy and every new video gets weak initial signal no matter how good it is. Topic consistency = clean seed data = stronger cascades.

**One topic, hammered to retirement** is the optimal strategy. Different topics = different channels.

### YouTube Expectations
- 3-5 year commitment minimum
- You don't need huge views — you need on-target views
- Every video is a sales agent working 24/7 that can reactivate at any time
- Shorts have a 72-hour half-life. YouTube is evergreen forever
- You can change titles and thumbnails months/years later and revive dead videos
- Growth loop is just slower because each iteration takes 5-7 days instead of 1

## 📊 AUDIENCE MATCHING & DISTRIBUTION (Core Framework)

**The algorithm has one job:** Keep people on the platform watching ads. To do that, it needs to know exactly who wants what content so it can serve them what keeps them watching. Narrow, consistent content = clean audience profile = better distribution.

Going wider doesn't get you more views as a function of broader audience — it gets you *muddier* initial sample data and worse cascade performance.

**On target views vs. viral views:** 
- Media game = need viral views, need to maximize total views, broadens topic aperture
- Business game = need on-target views, need dollars per view, narrows topic aperture
- Going wider to chase views is *antithetical* to dollars if you're selling a product/service

**Followers/subscribers:**
- Matter only as social proof past a minimum credibility threshold (Instagram: ~10k, YouTube: ~10k subs)
- Above that threshold, the difference between 180k and 365k followers is zero
- Only want new followers who are on-target to your offers
- Brand deals are the one exception where follower count matters (one of 3 pricing inputs)

**Term ownership:** The path to category cult fandom. When you own a niche term, people don't think of you as "one of 200 names" — they think of you *when the term is mentioned*. Own the niche terms nobody owns yet. "Business" is too big (no one person owns it). "AI factory robotics" — easier to own, and the brands in that category will pay a premium for access.

## 📧 LEAD MAGNETS & OWNING YOUR AUDIENCE

5 types of lead magnets (email in exchange for value):

1. **One-off PDF/doc** — "Here's my storytelling framework. Download it below." Easiest.
2. **3-5 day free email series** — Dripped over multiple days. His: Viralityblueprint.com
3. **Free community** — Give email to join. His: Wavy World (community + free curriculum)
4. **Waitlist/landing page**
5. **Form** (e.g., "Want our editors? Fill this out") — in description

**Why email matters:** Platforms are algorithm-controlled. Email is the one place you somewhat control distribution. 60% open rates are achievable. People buy from email. He believes you can build a 7-8 figure business with just content → lead magnet → email drip → offer.

**Native Embeds vs. Non-Native Ads:**
The goal of content is to hypnotize the viewer. A standard ad read breaks the hypnosis — they snap out of the trance, realize they're watching a video, and leave.

**Non-native ad:** Breaks away, new scene, change of energy, "we'll be right back from our sponsor." WORST thing you can do for retention.

**Native embed:** Weave the product/service into the conversation naturally so that it doesn't break the flow. Example: naturally mentioning his editors mid-conversation and saying "link below if you want them." That's a free ad that doesn't cost retention.

**Placement rule:** Your own native embeds go as early and as often as possible without alienating sponsors. Brand sponsor reads go *after* the AVD (average view duration) point — anyone who's still watching past that point has high purchase intent anyway, and you're not burning your hook audience on an ad.

## 🏆 EDITING MISTAKES TO AVOID

1. **Visual misalignment** — wrong visuals at wrong time, not matching the words
2. **Pacing off** — the eyes-closed test: close your eyes and just listen. Bored = too slow. "How can I absorb this?" = too fast. Find the sweet spot.
3. **Poor visual selection** — every sentence should have the *best possible* visual for it. Sometimes that's just your face. But if a visual adds absorption/clarity, use it.

**Eyes-closed test:** After you have a cut, close your eyes and just listen. If you're bored during any stretch — it's too slow. If it feels overwhelming — it's too fast. This will tell you more than your retention chart.

## 🎯 THE BIGGEST HACKS

**Short form #1 hack:** Look at your own content for winners first. If you don't have winners, study the best performers in your space with as many Lego bricks held constant as possible. Make your version. First 100 reps should be doing this — not reinventing the wheel.

**The comment strategy:** Use your script to drive a specific question that creates contrarian debate. "Little cyclones" in the comments boost engagement. Also: super accounts (high follower counts) liking/sharing your content may have weighted algorithmic value — their shares/likes could be 1,000x a normal account's action.

**Numbered series:** Creates a puzzle completion loop that forces bingeability. Human brain wants to complete puzzles — if you see Day 7 of 10, you go back for Days 1-6.

**Repackaging old YouTube videos:** Delete old video → wait 3 days (to sever YouTube's copyright memory) → re-edit, re-title, re-upload as new. It will be treated as fresh content.

**Content operator:** The single biggest workflow unlock for short form. Offloading the infinite-pit of idea generation frees up all your mental energy for what only you can do.

## 📋 QUICK REFERENCE CHECKLIST

For any short form video, before posting ask:
- [ ] Do all 3 hook components (visual/text/spoken) align?
- [ ] Does the visual stop the scroll (high motion, high color, high contrast)?
- [ ] Is context established in the first sentence?
- [ ] Is there clear contrast (direct preferred over implied)?
- [ ] Does each sentence open exactly one clear question?
- [ ] Is absorption rate at 100% through the whole piece?
- [ ] Are visuals correctly matched to every sentence?
- [ ] Is the pacing right (eyes-closed test)?
- [ ] Is there a dopamine hit in the first 30 seconds?
- [ ] Does the thumbnail work as a standalone static image (for DM shares)?

For YouTube, before hitting record ask:
- [ ] Is the title done and does it trigger a clear desire loop?
- [ ] Does the thumbnail amplify (not repeat) the title?
- [ ] Do the first 5 seconds confirm the desire loop from the thumbnail/title?
- [ ] Is there a dopamine hit within 60 seconds?
- [ ] Is the topic consistent with everything else on the channel?
